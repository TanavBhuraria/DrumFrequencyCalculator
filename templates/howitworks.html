How It Works

See drum physics in motion — the visualiser is the star.

Overview

This project is built around a single, interactive goal: make the physics of vibrating drumheads obvious and intuitive. The visualiser is the primary feature — a real‑time animation that turns equations into motion. A supporting drum frequency calculator provides theoretical pitch estimates so you can connect what you see with what you measure.

Wave Visualisation (Main Feature)

The visualiser animates membrane vibrations so you can explore how parameters change the motion. It is designed for experimentation and discovery:

Modes of vibration
  • Standing waves — nodes remain fixed while the membrane oscillates in place.
  • Traveling waves — the pattern moves across the surface, showing propagation.
  • Hybrid waves — a slow drift combined with oscillation, producing lifelike motion.
Harmonics

Add integer multiples of the base spatial frequency to see how overtones reshape the waveform. Each harmonic contributes additional wiggles and sharpens features — this is the same mechanism that gives real drums their timbre.

Energy visualization

Color maps show local energy (approximate kinetic + potential). Brighter regions indicate stronger motion or strain, helping you spot nodes, antinodes, and regions of concentrated activity.

Interactive controls

Sliders let you change amplitude, wavelength, frequency, drift, and harmonic gains. Zoom and pan across a long map to inspect the wave over a wide span.

Why the Visualiser Matters

Equations are compact but abstract. The visualiser translates those equations into movement you can see and manipulate. It helps you:

  • Understand how changing tension, size, or mass affects motion.
  • See how harmonics combine to form complex waveforms.
  • Relate energy distribution to audible behavior (where motion is strongest).
  • Experiment safely with extreme parameters while the renderer keeps visuals stable.

Frequency Calculator (Supporting Tool)

The calculator gives a theoretical estimate of the drumhead's fundamental pitch using a standard membrane model. It complements the visualiser by providing numbers you can compare with the animation.

The formula used is:

F = (v / 2π r) × √(T / μ)

Where:

  • F: Fundamental frequency (Hz)
  • v: Mode constant (≈ 2.405 for the first zero of J₀)
  • r: Radius of the drumhead (m)
  • T: Tension per unit length (N/m)
  • μ: Surface mass density (kg/m²)

Assumptions and Limitations

This model and the visualiser are intentionally simplified to highlight core physics. Key assumptions:

  • Perfectly circular, uniformly tensioned membrane
  • Edges are fixed (ideal boundary conditions)
  • Primary focus on the fundamental mode and low harmonics
  • No explicit damping or complex coupling to shells, air, or hardware

These simplifications make the system predictable and educational; real instruments will introduce additional effects (shell resonance, nonuniform tension, damping, coupling to air) that shift frequencies and timbre.

Applications

  • Musicians — visually tune and explore how adjustments affect motion and pitch.
  • Instrument makers — prototype how size, tension, and material choices influence behavior.
  • Students — connect Bessel functions and wave equations to observable motion.
  • Researchers — use the visualiser as a sandbox for hypothesis testing and model refinement.

Research & Validation

The project supports an experimental workflow: measure radius, tension, and membrane density; predict frequency with the calculator; record the actual sound; compare and iterate. Discrepancies point to real‑world effects (damping, nonuniform tension, shell coupling) that are valuable research targets.

Getting Started

Open the visualiser, then:

  • Set a moderate amplitude and wavelength to begin.
  • Toggle between Standing, Traveling, and Hybrid to see different behaviors.
  • Enable harmonics one at a time to observe how each overtone changes the waveform.
  • Turn on energy visualization to locate nodes and high‑motion regions.
  • Use the frequency calculator to estimate the fundamental pitch and compare with the visual motion.

The visualiser is the heart of this site — spend time with it. Move sliders slowly, watch how patterns evolve, and use the calculator to ground what you see in numbers.
